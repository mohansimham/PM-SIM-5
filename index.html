Before You Begin

A quick refresher on the frameworks you'll apply in this simulation.

🌍

Market Sizing

Estimating the size of the opportunity before you invest

Market sizing uses TAM (Total Addressable Market), SAM (Serviceable Available Market), and SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market) to estimate how big an opportunity really is. Bottom-up estimation builds from unit-level data; top-down starts from industry totals.

How to apply it

Start bottom-up: How many potential users? What would each pay? How often? Multiply up. Then sanity-check top-down against industry reports. Ask: Is this market growing or shrinking? What share can we realistically capture in year 1 vs. year 5?

Example

Uber entering a new city: How many daily commuters? What % would switch from taxis? At what average fare? Bottom-up gives a realistic SOM — not the fantasy TAM investors love to quote.

Unit Economics

Does each transaction make or lose money?

Unit economics measures the direct revenues and costs of a single unit of your business — typically one customer or one transaction. Key metrics: CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost), LTV (Lifetime Value), and contribution margin. If LTV > CAC, you have a viable business. If not, growth just accelerates losses.

How to apply it

Calculate: What does it cost to acquire one customer? How much revenue do they generate over their lifetime? What's the payback period? Ask: Are we subsidising growth or building a sustainable model? What levers can improve LTV or reduce CAC?

Example

Uber spends heavily on driver and rider incentives to grow. If a rider's LTV is $500 but CAC is $50, that's 10x return. But if subsidies push CAC to $400, the model breaks — growth becomes a cash bonfire.

5 Whys Analysis

Digging to the root cause, not treating symptoms

The 5 Whys is a root cause analysis technique: ask "why" five times to drill past symptoms to the underlying problem. Originally from Toyota's production system, it's invaluable for product teams facing operational failures, user churn, or quality issues.

How to apply it

Start with the problem: "Driver cancellation rates are up 20%." Why? "Drivers are rejecting short trips." Why? "Short trips don't cover their costs." Why? Keep going until you hit a systemic issue you can actually fix — not just a band-aid.

Example

Uber sees surge pricing complaints spike. Why? Prices 5x normal. Why? Not enough drivers. Why? Drivers avoid certain areas. Why? Safety concerns. The fix isn't capping surge — it's addressing driver safety.

Competitive Strategy

Winning in winner-take-all markets

Competitive strategy in ride-hailing is about network effects, pricing power, and market entry timing. In winner-take-most markets, being first matters — but being best in the right segments matters more. Strategy here means choosing where to compete and how aggressively.

How to apply it

Ask: Is this a winner-take-all or winner-take-most market? What's our moat — brand, network effects, cost structure? Should we match competitor pricing or differentiate on experience? When does aggressive expansion destroy value instead of creating it?

Example

Uber enters a market where Ola dominates. Subsidise rides to steal share? Launch a premium tier to avoid a price war? Focus on underserved segments like airport rides? Each path has a different competitive logic.
